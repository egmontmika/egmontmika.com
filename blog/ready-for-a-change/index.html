Ready for a Change?

Have you ever tried to change someone’s habit? You kept arguing, but it didn’t help?

Or have you tried to convince yourself and then change an old habit according to your own arguments? Like introducing a new diet or a daily workout or a regular time for study and prayer, or making it a habit to always be on time? Or…?

Did it work out for you? And in case it did, did you succeed because someone kept bombing you with all the good arguments?

My experience is that whenever I managed to establish a new habit, I first landed in a crisis, or was desperate enough or just fed up with the status quo. Or maybe filled with an exciting vision.

Deep inside, I so badly wanted a change, no matter what the reasoning, and long before I knew all the arguments. And even long before my mind was completely clear about what I really wanted.

We probably change a habit on other grounds than logical thinking. Yet, once the decision is made, we welcome arguments to back it up with, and we use these arguments whenever we feel a need to motivate ourselves or defend our decision.

Real change is made on a much deeper, emotional or maybe rather spiritual level. It’s the level where we manage things like courage and fear, trust and distrust, faith and doubt.

It seems to me like most of us, most of the time are not really ready for a change. Although we may not be quite happy about a certain habit, we still prefer the status quo. It just feels safer or easier; at least we know what we got and we hope that it will not get worse.

While on the outside listening to all the good arguments and nodding our heads in agreement, our inside may scream a roaring NO. While our intellect agrees, our inside is already blocking the change.

This is the situation every preacher is facing. Probably, most of us listeners are not really willing to change. And for not exposing our half-heartedness, or in order to not offend anyone with a clear ”no”, we escape into pretending. However, true preaching aims at change; changed attitudes, decisions and actions - including changed habits.

How can we go on listening week after week and still stay calm?

The explanation is that listening without acting leads into ritualized, passive behavior. And listening to still more leads into even deeper passivity. It immunizes against the message, makes you favor the status quo and end up in self-affirmation and convenience, the perfect setting for a religious behavior.

Then, listening to sermons becomes counterproductive. It makes you feel spiritual without being spiritual.

We fool ourselves when we think of having accomplished anything by just listening or just by agreeing to all the arguments.

On the other hand, in case we are honest and willing to change, good arguments can be quite useful. They can back up our decision and make it really strong and effective.

Are you ready for a change?
